A few years ago I started keeping a plain-text file on my desktop called `log.md`. Every time I learned something — a shortcut, a mental model, a line of code that finally made sense — I'd add a bullet. No formatting rules, no categories, just date and note.

I expected it to become a searchable reference. What I didn't expect was how much the *act of writing* would change the way I learn.

## Writing forces a decision

When you take a note, you have to decide what actually matters. Most of what I consume day-to-day — articles, docs, videos — evaporates within hours. The log creates a small friction point: *is this worth writing down?* That question alone filters out a lot of noise before anything hits the page.

## Re-reading is cheap, re-learning is not

The compound interest on a log shows up months later. I'll hit a problem, vaguely remember solving something adjacent, search the file for a keyword, and find exactly the note I needed. Without the log I would have spent an hour re-deriving something I already knew.

## It's a record of your taste changing

Looking back at entries from two years ago is genuinely interesting. The things I flagged as surprising then are obvious to me now — which is a reliable signal that I've actually internalized them. And occasionally I'll find a note that still surprises me, which is a reminder there's more to do.

---

If you don't already keep something like this, start with the lowest possible barrier: one file, one line per entry, no rules. The habit matters more than the format.
